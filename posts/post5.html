05 Essay 16 March 2026

Who writes dialogue like this anymore?

There is a line in Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man where Tommy Shelby says: "I wrote it all down. Everything." He is talking about his memoir. Seven words. No adjectives. No metaphor. No explanation of what "everything" means. He trusts you to fill in thirteen years of violence and grief yourself.

That is screenwriting. The stuff you leave out.

Steven Knight writes dialogue like a boxer throws punches - most of the work is in the feints. Tommy almost never says what he means. He says something next to what he means, and the person across from him has to figure out if they are being threatened, tested, or forgiven. It is impossibly hard to write. Murphy delivers it like he was born speaking this way. Watch the pub scene - the grenade scene. Tommy does not explain his rage. He does not deliver a monologue about respect. He just acts. The silence around the action is where the meaning lives.

Now compare that to The Bride!. Every character says exactly what they feel. At full volume. All the time. When the Bride screams about the injustice of being brought back to life without her consent - that is the film's thesis, stated directly to camera. No subtext. Just text. It is bracing for five minutes. It is numbing for two hours. Gyllenhaal writes like she does not trust the audience to infer anything, so she underlines it, bolds it, and reads it aloud.

Goddard, in Project Hail Mary, does something smarter. He gives Ryland Grace the voice of a teacher - a man who instinctively explains things. Then he takes away everyone Grace can explain things to. Grace talks to himself. He talks to Rocky in improvised pidgin. He narrates his own panic. The dialogue is functional, often funny, never literary. It sounds like a real person under pressure - not a character performing intelligence for the audience.

The best screen dialogue disappears. Nobody walks out of a cinema and says "the dialogue was structurally sound." But when it is wrong - when characters speak in thesis statements, or deliver exposition like they are reading footnotes aloud - you feel it. You cannot name it. But you feel it. And you reach for your phone.
