Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man - a beautiful, frustrating goodbye

Let me tell you what Tommy Shelby meant to people.

He meant: wear the suit. Walk like you own the room. Protect your family at all costs. Trust yourself first, everyone else second. Check behind your shoulder. Do not flinch. Do not explain. Let them wonder what you are thinking. He was not a good man. That was the point. He was a precise man. A man with a code - ugly and violent, but a code nonetheless. And for six seasons, Cillian Murphy played him like a blade wrapped in wool.

Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man gives us Tommy Shelby at 50-something, hiding in a rotting country house, writing his memoirs and being haunted by visions of his dead daughter. He has lost. He knows he has lost. And Steven Knight's screenplay wants us to watch him accept that loss with grace and finally die in peace.

I do not buy it.

The Tommy Shelby I watched for six seasons would not rot in a farmhouse. He would burn it down and build something on the ashes. The man who outplayed the Italian mob, the Russians, Oswald Mosley and the British government does not retire to write a book. That is not the character. That is the writer being tired of the character.

Barry Keoghan plays Duke - Tommy's illegitimate son, now running the Peaky Blinders with all of his father's violence and none of his brain. Keoghan is perfectly cast. He plays Duke like a kid wearing his dad's coat and finding it too heavy. There is a scene in a pub - Tommy pulls a grenade, stuffs it down a man's shirt, kicks him out the door - and the audience cheered. They should not have. That scene is Knight testing whether we still worship Tommy even when he is clearly the monster. We do. We always will. That is the problem Knight cannot solve.

The plot involves Nazis. Counterfeit money. Operation Bernhard - a real scheme to collapse the British economy with fake banknotes. Tim Roth plays the fascist handler. He is a cartoon. Rebecca Ferguson plays a Romani mystic. She is wasted. The absence of Helen McCrory - Polly Gray, the actual soul of the show, lost when McCrory died in 2021 - is a wound the film pretends it can stitch with new characters. It cannot.

There is a scene where Tommy rides a horse through a burning Birmingham to the sound of Nick Cave. It is either the most absurd or the most beautiful thing I have seen this year. I cannot decide. That uncertainty is Peaky Blinders at its best: too stylish to dismiss, too ridiculous to take seriously.

The ending - Tommy dying in the mud, reciting a hymn, his son pulling the trigger because his father asked him to - is genuinely moving. Murphy earns every second of it. Thirteen years in this role and he still finds new ways to break your heart with his eyes.

But this is not the Tommy Shelby who taught a generation of men to stand up straight. This is a Tommy who gave up. And watching a lion lie down and ask to be put out of its misery is not catharsis. It is just sad.

92% on Rotten Tomatoes. 59 on Metacritic. Those two numbers tell you everything about who liked this film and who understood it.
