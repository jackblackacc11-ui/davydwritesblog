Adaptation is a knife fight - and most writers lose

Every year Hollywood adapts beloved novels and every year readers complain the film is not faithful enough. This is boring. Faithfulness is a dead metric. A faithful adaptation of a 600-page novel is a 12-hour miniseries. A film is two hours. Something has to die.

The question is not: did they keep my favourite scene? The question is: did the writer find the right thing to kill?

Drew Goddard found it in Project Hail Mary. Andy Weir's novel is dense with hard science - pages of orbital mechanics, biochemistry, fuel calculations. It is also a story about a lonely man who makes a friend. Goddard cut the science to its essentials and let the friendship carry the weight. He restructured the timeline so the film opens with amnesia and feeds you information through flashbacks. He killed scenes that Weir fans will miss. He did not care. He should not have cared. His job was not to photocopy the book. His job was to make a film that works as a film.

Steven Knight had a different problem with Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man. He was not adapting someone else's novel. He was compressing his own six-season television show into a two-hour shape. The trick - and it mostly works - is ruthless narrowing. The film is not about the Peaky Blinders. It is about a father and a son. Everything else - the Nazis, the counterfeiting, the Blitz - is furniture arranged around that central relationship. Cut the furniture and the room collapses. But at least you know where to sit.

Maggie Gyllenhaal did the opposite. The Bride! does not adapt one source - it adapts all of them. Mary Shelley's novel. The 1935 film. The feminist implications. The horror genre. The crime thriller. The musical. She picked up every knife in the drawer and swung at everything. The result is a screenplay that cuts in seventeen directions and wounds nothing deeply.

Here is what nobody tells you about adaptation: the scenes you love most in the source material are usually the ones that have to go. The quiet moment that gave the book its soul but would murder the film's pace. The backstory that felt essential on page 40 but on screen becomes the minute the audience checks their phone. The subplot that enriches a novel across 400 pages but in a cinema is just a distraction from the thing you came to see.

The best adaptations are acts of violence committed with love. You can feel Goddard's love for Weir's book. You can also feel him reaching for the knife.
