Project Hail Mary is the film we needed - and twenty minutes too long

Here is the pitch: a middle school science teacher wakes up alone on a spaceship. His crewmates are dead. He cannot remember his own name. Earth is dying. He has to fix it.

That is a great pitch. Drew Goddard - the man who adapted Andy Weir's last space novel into The Martian - knows it is a great pitch. He also knows something most screenwriters forget: the science is not why anyone cares. The loneliness is why anyone cares. The science is just the thing the lonely person does so they do not go insane.

Ryan Gosling plays Ryland Grace. He is good. He is always good. But Gosling has this problem - he is almost too charming. He makes everything look easy, and easy is the enemy of tension. His best moments are not in space. They are in the flashback sequences on Earth, where he plays Grace as a nervous teacher being bullied into saving the world by Sandra Huller's Eva Stratt - a woman so coldly competent she makes you want to confess to crimes you have not committed. Huller does not raise her voice once in this entire film. She terrifies me more than the dying sun.

Then Rocky shows up. Rocky is an alien. He looks like a spider made of rock. He communicates through musical chords. He should be ridiculous. He is the best character in the film. James Ortiz performs him through puppetry - real, physical, on-set puppetry - and the result is something CG could never touch. When Rocky learns that Grace might leave him to die alone, and the only sound he makes is a single low chord - the entire cinema went quiet. Actual silence. In 2026. That does not happen anymore.

So where does it fall apart? The last twenty minutes. Goddard cannot find the exit. The film hits its emotional peak - Grace choosing to stay - and then keeps going. And going. Sandra Huller sings a full karaoke version of Harry Styles' "Sign of the Times" as a farewell scene. It was apparently her idea. I believe that. It feels exactly like an actor's idea that a director should have killed. Phil Lord and Christopher Miller - directing together for the first time since 22 Jump Street - let the sentimentality pile up until the ending feels less like a conclusion and more like a man who will not leave a party.

But here is the thing. When this film works - and it works for about two hours and ten minutes - it works completely. A schoolteacher and a rock save two planets. That sentence should not make you cry. The film will make you cry. 95% on Rotten Tomatoes does not happen by accident.

Cut twenty minutes. Lose the Harry Styles. You would have a masterpiece. What you have instead is just very, very good. In a spring full of sequels - I will take it.
