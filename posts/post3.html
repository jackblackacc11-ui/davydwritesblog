The Bride! has a hundred ideas and finishes none of them

The Bride! opens with the ghost of Mary Shelley speaking directly to camera. She is angry. She has unfinished business. She possesses a woman called Ida. Ida dies. Ida is dug up and reanimated as the Bride of Frankenstein. The Bride speaks in uncontrollable monologues, vomits black goo, accidentally starts a feminist revolution in 1930s Chicago, and goes on a crime spree with Frankenstein's monster - who, by the way, likes going to the cinema.

None of that is a joke. That is the actual plot.

Maggie Gyllenhaal wrote and directed this. Her debut - The Lost Daughter - was one of the most controlled, precise films of 2021. Three Oscar nominations. Not a single wasted frame. So what happened?

What happened is that nobody told her no. You can feel it in every scene. Every idea she had made it into the script. Bonnie and Clyde homage? In. Metropolis reference? In. Musical number? In. Jake Gyllenhaal doing a Fred Astaire impression? In. Two detective characters who belong in a completely different film? In. Feminist manifesto delivered to camera? In. Black goo? Definitely in.

Jessie Buckley plays three roles - Shelley, Ida, the Bride. She gives everything. She screams, convulses, dances, vomits, delivers monologues at full volume without breathing. It is physically astonishing. It is also - after ninety minutes - completely exhausting. When a performance has no quiet, the loud stops meaning anything.

Christian Bale gets it. He plays Frankenstein's monster - just "Frank" now - as gentle, confused, yearning. He barely speaks. He watches movies in empty cinemas with tears on his stitched-up face. Every scene he shares with Buckley crackles with tenderness. There is a version of this film that lives in those quiet moments - the monster and his bride figuring out what it means to exist without permission. That film would be devastating.

This film cost $90 million. It made $29 million. Those numbers are not a tragedy - they are a review. The audience is telling you something: ambition without discipline is noise. Gyllenhaal has the talent. She proved that. But The Lost Daughter worked because it was about one woman's very specific pain, told with restraint. The Bride! is about everything - motherhood, creation, bodily autonomy, feminism, Hollywood, crime, romance, the afterlife - and because it is about everything, it is about nothing.

The lesson is simple. A film is not a notebook. You do not put every idea in. You put the right ones in and you kill the rest. Gyllenhaal will make a great film again. This is not it.
